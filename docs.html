Starbucks Drink Nutrition Explorer – Documentation

Design notes, rationale, and findings for my D3 final project.

Back to visualization

1. Dataset

I used the public Starbucks drink menu nutrition dataset from Kaggle. It contains nutrition information for different Starbucks drinks and sizes. I focused on a subset of attributes:

  • Beverage_category – drink category (e.g., Coffee, Frappuccino).
  • Beverage – drink name.
  • Beverage_prep – preparation / size description.
  • Calories – calories per serving.
  • Sugars (g) – sugar in grams.
  • Caffeine (mg) – caffeine in milligrams (when present).

2. Design process

My main question was: “How do calories and sugar relate across different Starbucks drink categories?” I also wanted to see which drinks are both very sugary and high in calories, and how caffeine fits into the picture.

I started with quick pencil sketches of different options: one idea was multiple small bar charts per category, and another was a single scatterplot with filters. I decided to go with a scatterplot because it makes it easier to see the relationship between two continuous variables (calories and sugar).

I also sketched the layout: controls at the top, the main chart on the left, and a short “How to read” panel on the right so the page still feels like a dashboard and not just a naked chart.

3. Visualization & interactions

The main visualization is a scatterplot with:

  • X‑axis: Calories
  • Y‑axis: Sugar (grams)
  • Point color:
    • By default: drink category (categorical colors).
    • Optional: caffeine level, using a yellow→red gradient.
  • Interactions:
    • Category dropdown to filter to a single drink type or see all.
    • Slider to limit the maximum calories (e.g., “show me drinks under 300 calories”).
    • Dropdown to switch color encoding from category to caffeine level.
    • Tooltips on hover showing full drink name, prep, calories, sugar, and caffeine.

I capped the slider at 600 calories to avoid one or two extreme drinks stretching the scale too much. This keeps the chart visually stable even when you move the slider.

4. Design choices & rationale

4.1 Encoding

  • Scatterplot: Chosen because it directly supports my main analytical task: visually comparing calories versus sugar.
  • Color:
    • Category colors use D3’s schemeTableau10, which is colorblind‑friendly and readable.
    • Caffeine uses a sequential yellow→red scale so “hotter” colors feel like higher caffeine.
  • Tooltip: Instead of labeling points directly (which would be messy), I kept labels in a hover tooltip.

4.2 Layout

  • Controls live in a horizontal strip at the top so they feel like filters for the whole view.
  • The explanatory notes on the right help a casual user understand what the chart is doing without needing to read the assignment.

5. Findings

Using the visualization I noticed several patterns:

  • Many Frappuccino‑style drinks cluster in the high‑calorie, high‑sugar top‑right of the chart.
  • Brewed coffee and plain espresso drinks are usually very low in both calories and sugar unless they’re mixed with milk and syrups.
  • Some drinks are high in sugar but not especially high in caffeine, which is easy to see when coloring by caffeine level.

These patterns are the kind of relationship that would be tedious to dig out from rows in a spreadsheet, but they become obvious once you can filter and hover through the scatterplot.
